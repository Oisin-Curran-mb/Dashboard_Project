Purpose
Shows how the organisation's actual income or spending compares to what was budgeted, across each period of the financial year — both period-by-period and as a running year-to-date total — so users can quickly see where they're ahead of or behind plan.
Customer Research — What We're Working Off
business_center
Bold BI — Budget vs Actual Dashboard open_in_new
A grouped bar chart (Budget vs Actual side by side, per period) is the standard first view for this exact comparison — the direct reason Variance Bar is the default view here. Bold BI's sample dashboard is the one-customer reference used for that call; Zebra BI and Domo were the separate sources used for the Waterfall alternate view and the KPI-strip placement (see Logic, below).
Logic — What This Shows, and Why We Built It This Way

The chart compares Budget to Actual for each period of the fiscal year, as dollar amounts, so a bar sitting above or below its neighbour reads immediately as ahead of or behind plan, no subtraction required (see the 2026-07-09 fix below — this used to mislabel these dollar figures as a percentage). The KPI strip above the chart (Medium and Large only) restates the same comparison as numbers: all four figures on Large, trimmed to just Variance and % Used on Medium since there isn't room for all four without crowding the chart itself. Small drops the strip entirely and caps at 4 periods — it's a glance-sized tile, not a reporting view.

Budget-vs-actual reporting is one of the most standardised chart problems in financial dashboards, so nothing here is a novel choice: a grouped bar chart is the standard first view for this comparison, and a cumulative waterfall is the recommended second view for showing how period variances build to a year-end total — why Waterfall was kept as a full alternate view instead of being cut. Green/red variance colouring is the universal convention across every source reviewed, carried through here and in the Data Table view.

Two fixes made as part of this check: (1) the KPI header strip was previously only wired up on the Horizontal Bar List alternate view (Option B); it's now added to this default Variance Bar view's Medium/Large sizes too, matching what the locked design doc actually calls for. (2) the dedicated KPI-size card (1×0.5) was showing YTD Actual as its headline instead of the documented Variance ($), colour-coded green/red — also fixed, and now consistent with Option B's KPI card. The GL drill-through link is still pending backend confirmation — tracked separately in the developer punch list, not a gap in this widget's design.

Round 2 fixes (2026-07-09). KPI and Small card titles now read "Variance — [Account Type], [Fiscal Year]" instead of a static chart name, reusing the same generic headline function built for W02 (now extended to support more than one card size). Weekly was added as a real, selectable Period View option (previously missing from the option list entirely, despite being in the locked doc) — it's scoped to Large/Expanded in the Change Filters modal, matching the doc's "Large/Expanded only" restriction; the underlying weekly transaction-grain question is still a backend open item, unaffected by this mock-side fix.

Round 3 fixes (2026-07-09, same day). The KPI sparkline hover from Round 2 wasn't actually triggering — it used an SVG <title> child element, which doesn't reliably fire a hover tooltip on freshly-injected inline SVG here; switched to a title attribute instead, the same mechanism the grouped-bar hovers elsewhere already use successfully. The KPI-label definition bubbles from Round 2 were also getting clipped at the card's left edge for YTD Budget/Variance (the left-column labels) — they were centred on the trigger, so a narrow card had nowhere to put the left half; now anchored to the trigger's own left/right edge so they always grow inward. '% Used' (and Variance) wording was rewritten to drop the "YTD" shorthand from the explanation itself, checked against how budgeting sources plainly explain a %-of-budget figure.

Two real, confirmed bugs were also found and fixed while re-verifying the data for the above: (1) Budget/Actual per period are dollar amounts, not a percentage — the table, bar chart tooltips, and axis titles across all three views were mislabelling and displaying them as "78%" instead of "$78k"; all now show dollar amounts, with Variance additionally showing a % of that period's budget since it's the one figure that legitimately works either way. Quarterly's grouping was switched from averaging to summing months to match (averaging was only ever valid under the old, wrong %-of-budget reading). Recomputing exact sums also caught two smaller data bugs along the way: FY2026's totals didn't quite match its own periods for any Account Type (now corrected, and a partial 'Jul' period was added since FY2026 was also missing its current in-progress month entirely — that's why Quarterly could never show more than Q1+Q2 before; it now shows a real, honestly-low Q3), and Custom Report's FY2025/FY2024 totals were each exactly $100k too high, an unrelated data-entry typo. (2) This doc has always said "green = favourable (under budget on expenses / over on income)," but the KPI headline, table rows, cumulative variance, and Option B's bar chart never implemented that flip — they coloured green whenever Actual ≥ Budget regardless of Account Type, backwards for Expense Accounts/Custom Report. Now correctly flips at every level.

Every card on this page — KPI, Small, Medium, Large — is the real widget card, not a simplified stand-in: same badge, chart name/subtitle, eye (purpose) button, expand button, and 3-dot menu (download, filters, chart-type switch, size) as the live Dashboard tab. The eye and expand buttons disappear at KPI size and the chart-type switch disappears at KPI/Small — both deliberate, matching the locked size-behaviour table, not missing chrome. Size buttons here resize that card only, independent of the other three, so you can compare all four side by side; Change filters and chart-type switching are shared with the Dashboard tab's own state, since Account Type/Fiscal Year and the current view are genuinely global to Option A in the real app, not per-size. Caveat: because "Change filters" on every Final Check card size (KPI/Small/Medium/Large) delegates to the same modal the real Dashboard tab's Option A card uses, the Weekly-at-Large-only trim reads that real card's current size, not whichever Final Check card you clicked from — worth a manual check once this is wired into the real gallery.

Purpose
Gives a clear overview of how much is being contributed annually across each pension plan type, with the ability to filter by church district, and lets users drill into individual appointees per plan.
Customer Research — What We're Working Off
business_center
Mercer — Plan Dashboard open_in_new
No named competitor benchmarks this exact district × plan-type combination — it's a narrower, org-specific slice than most benefits dashboards cover. Donut/pie for a proportional split is the standard choice when the question narrows to a single dimension (plan type, ignoring district) — the direct basis for Pie by Plan Type now being the default. Grouped/stacked bar charts remain the applicable standard when comparing two dimensions at once (district × plan type), which is why that chart is kept as the Switch Chart Type alternate rather than dropped.
Logic — What This Shows, and Why We Built It This Way

The default chart is a pie split by plan type — Defined Benefit, Defined Contribution, 403(b) — showing the org's overall plan mix at a glance, at every size including Small. Switching to Grouped Bar by District brings back the two-dimension view (district × plan type, stacked at Small for space, side-by-side at Medium/Large) for when the question is "who costs what," rather than "what's our plan mix." Both charts read the same Church District/Plan Type filters, so switching between them never changes what data is being looked at, only how it's drawn.

Changed on direct instruction (2026-07-09). The locked doc previously made Grouped Bar by District the default, with Donut by Plan Type as a secondary view and no Switch View at all at Small. That's been reversed here: Pie is now the default at every size, Grouped Bar is the alternate, and Switch Chart Type is now offered at Small too — all three cards above have it. The doc has been updated to match; see its Views section for the new wording.

Large now shows the chart and the Summary Table together — stacked vertically, both driven by the same filters — instead of Table being a third item in the Switch Chart Type menu. This replaces the doc's previous "Active view + table toggle" behaviour at Large with "chart and table shown together, always." Small stays chart-only. Medium now also offers a standalone Data Table via Switch Chart Type (added on direct instruction) — selecting it replaces the chart with the same District × Plan Type table Large shows, but on its own rather than paired with the chart, since Medium doesn't have the room for both at once.

Filter behaviour, corrected (2026-07-09). Selecting a specific Church District (leaving Plan Type on All Plans) narrows the pie to that one district's plan-type breakdown — this direction was already correct, confirmed by tracing the mock data, which keeps a dedicated bucket per district. Selecting a specific Plan Type, however, was a real bug: the pie used to render a single 100% slice for that one plan type instead of anything useful. It now switches the pie's slicing dimension to District — showing how that one plan is spread across Central/North/South/East/West — which is what "that plan across the district" means. Grouped Bar and the Data Table were already correct in this case (they've always broken things down by district), so only the default Pie branch needed the fix.

KPI card title now reflects the active filters (added 2026-07-09). It reads "Overall Contribution" when Church District and Plan Type are both at their "All ..." default, the district name alone when only District is narrowed, the plan type alone when only Plan Type is narrowed, and "District by Plan Type" when both are — built with a new generic function, fcKpiFilterHeadline(), rather than anything W02-specific, so the same pattern can be dropped onto another widget's KPI card later without rebuilding the truncation/tooltip/fallback logic from scratch. Long results truncate with "…" and show the full text on hover (reusing the ellipsis rule every card title already has).

One caveat: the "Switch Chart Type available at Small" change is implemented here in the WRENDER logic and on this Final Check page. The real dashboard gallery's Small-size dropdown markup for this widget lives in a large static block of HTML that this session's tooling couldn't safely inspect or edit tonight (see the review list) — worth a manual check that the same menu option appears there too.

Purpose
Shows a breakdown of payroll already paid within a chosen date range, helping users understand how total payroll spend is distributed across pay-type categories such as regular hours, overtime, and paid leave. This is a post-payroll check of what went out, not a forward-looking view of what's owed — updated 2026-07-16, per direct instruction, to correct that framing.
Customer Research — What We're Working Off
business_center
Figma Community — Free Payroll Dashboard open_in_new
Pie/donut breakdowns only work up to ~4-5 categories before bar charts become the easier read — which is why both a Bar and a Donut view are kept as peers here rather than picking one, since the right default depends on how many compensation categories a given organisation actually has. This Figma payroll dashboard is the closest reference for the horizontal-bar default; a separate source (Acciyo) is the basis for pairing the breakdown with the Period Comparison trend view, kept as a third full peer.
Logic — What This Shows, and Why We Built It This Way

Current design. One row per breakdown item, sized by amount — Horizontal Bars is the default because it scales cleanly regardless of category count (Donut/Pie only reads well up to ~4-5 slices). When Department = All Departments, each row is one of the four departments (staff groups) with that department's total; picking a specific department drills into its own pay-type breakdown — Regular, Vacation, OverTime, Sick, Double Time, Personal, Holiday, Misc, Other (changed 2026-07-16, per direct instruction — see the note below) — instead. In the All-Departments Bar view, each department's bar is now a stack of its own pay-type segments rather than one flat colour (added 2026-07-16, per direct instruction) — so the composition behind each department's total is visible at a glance, with a shared legend below; drilled into one department, bars are already that breakdown, so they stay plain single-colour. Bar, Pie Chart and Data Table all read this exact same row set, so switching between them changes only how it's drawn, never what's being counted. Every row also shows a ▲/▼ % change against the same-length prior period (on the bar, and as a Change column on the table) — a raw total doesn't say whether costs are trending up or down, so the comparison is always visible, not tucked behind a separate view. Small shows the top 3 rows, Medium the top 5, Large all of them plus totals; the KPI headline is Total Payroll Amount for the selected period. Switch Chart Type (Bar/Pie/Table) is available at every size except KPI, including Small.

Filters. Department narrows every view to one department's payroll. Pay Period is Weekly/Bi-Weekly/Monthly/Custom: the three presets anchor on a Pay Date field (the date payroll is actually issued) and count backward from it — Weekly = 7 days, Bi-Weekly = 14, Monthly = 30 (a day-count approximation, not calendar-month-aware — open question, not guessed at). Custom instead reveals its own explicit Beginning/Ending date fields and ignores Pay Date entirely. A "Make this recurring" checkbox sits alongside the preset periods, and a second checkbox, "Set Pay Period separately per department," replaces the single shared Pay Date with one Start Date + Period + Make recurring block per department (Weekly/Bi-Weekly/Monthly only — Custom isn't offered per-department), each block separated by a divider line. The resolved end date shows above the card as "Paid: date" (with a 🔁 if recurring); the table view additionally shows the full range as "Period shown: start – end" on the table grid. When Separate By Department is on and Department = All Departments, there's no single shared date to show up top, so the callout says as much and each department's own row carries its own period + recurring indicator instead — a Period column on the table, subtext under each bar, and a small per-department list under the pie.

Note — "paid," not "owed" (2026-07-16, per direct instruction). This widget reports payroll that has already been paid within the selected period — it's an after-the-fact check of what went out, not a forward-looking view of an outstanding obligation. That's an important correction to how this was framed: the "Payment due" callout above every view used to name the pay date as if it were something still owed, which is backwards — the pay date shown is always in the past or present, never a future amount due. Relabelled to "Paid: date" everywhere it appears (the top-of-card callout, the per-department rows, and this doc). The current-vs-prior comparison isn't affected by this correction — it's still comparing two already-paid periods against each other, which is exactly the kind of after-the-fact check this widget is for.

Note — pay-type categories replace the paycheck-stub breakdown (2026-07-16, per direct instruction). Drilling into a specific department used to show Net Pay/Fed Tax/Benefits/State Tax/Retirement/Overtime — a gross-to-net breakdown of one paycheck's line items, more like reading a pay stub top to bottom. That's not the right lens here. It's now Regular, Vacation, OverTime, Sick, Double Time, Personal, Holiday, Misc, Other — the pay-type/earnings codes hours are actually coded under on a pay run, which is what "breaking down a pay group" should mean. This should be linkable in the real system — pay groups already tie to compensation records — but the exact field or table isn't confirmed; the original Purpose doc's formula (`SUM(PRHistoryCompensation.Amount) GROUP BY CompensationDistributionID, Name`) groups by compensation distribution, not by pay type, so there's likely a different dimension (an earnings/pay-type code) somewhere off `PRHistoryCompensation` or a related detail table that this widget would need instead. Flagged for the developer to dig into rather than assumed — see the Developer Punch List.

Compared to the current real system (Purpose doc): today's widget is a single fixed layout — a table and a pie chart shown side by side, always grouped by compensation category, with plain Beginning/Ending date pickers (defaulting 1 Jan–today), no comparison to any other period, no per-department scheduling of any kind, no recurring option, no drill-down, and no department filter. This redesign changes all of that: Bar is the new default (pie/donut alone doesn't hold up once an org has more than ~5 categories), Pie and Table are kept as full peers rather than dropped; a Department filter and department-level rollup were added since HR/finance review is usually department-first, and — since different departments can genuinely run on different pay schedules — that filter now optionally splits into fully independent per-department periods rather than forcing one shared date on every department; the raw date pickers became named, Pay-Date-anchored periods, with an explicit recurring flag, since a payroll total only means something in the context of an actual pay run, not an arbitrary calendar range; and a current-vs-prior comparison was added everywhere because "what did we spend" is a weaker question than "is this drifting." A drill-through to the Payroll History module is specced as a genuinely new feature — the current system has no drill-down of any kind — and Department is a new filter the current system's data source doesn't clearly support yet (see Open Items below).

Open items — not built, flagged rather than guessed at: (1) Department field is not confirmed to exist on the real `PRHistory`/`PRHistoryCompensation` tables — backend needs to confirm before this filter can go live. (2) Drill-through target page (the Payroll History module) isn't confirmed yet. (3) The Data Table Sort rule (fixed alphabetical, with a user toggle to Amount descending) — only the fixed alphabetical default is built; the toggle isn't. (4) Monthly's 30-day approximation vs. a true calendar-month subtraction, noted above. (5) "Make this recurring" is stored and displayed (including per-department) but is a flag only in this mockup — nothing here actually advances a period forward over calendar time; that's real scheduling logic for the backend, not something a static mock can demonstrate. (6) Custom isn't offered as a per-department option — only Weekly/Bi-Weekly/Monthly — flagged as a scope decision rather than an oversight, since a fully independent custom range per department starts to look like four separate widgets. (7) The new pay-type breakdown (Regular/Vacation/OverTime/Sick/Double Time/Personal/Holiday/Misc/Other) is mock data only — it should be linkable via pay groups, but the real field/table backing it isn't confirmed; needs developer investigation before build.

Purpose
Shows how well the organisation is keeping up with its remittance pledge commitments. For each activity type, users can see what was pledged, how much has been paid, what remains outstanding, and the percentage paid so far.
Customer Research — What We're Working Off
business_center
Virtuous — Pledge Tracking open_in_new
No dedicated competitor product for denominational apportionment/remittance tracking exists — this is a niche church-finance concept — so the benchmark is the general nonprofit "Pledge vs. Received" pattern instead of one named direct competitor. That pattern has an established fulfillment-rate formula (Received ÷ Pledged × 100, healthy range 85–95%, per DonorSearch), which is the direct reason overall % Paid is the KPI headline. Virtuous's pledge-tracking approach is the closest reference for the progress-bar default.
Logic — What This Shows, and Why We Built It This Way

One filled bar per activity type — the filled portion is what's been received, the empty remainder is outstanding, so the gap itself is visible at a glance without reading numbers. Progress Bars is the default because it's the single most common visualisation for this exact "pledge vs received" metric across every source reviewed.

Small shows 2-3 activity types with no Switch View — a glance-sized read. Medium and Large show the active view (bars or table) across 4-5 or all activity types respectively, with full values and %. The KPI headline is overall % Paid — received divided by pledged across every activity type — the named industry fulfilment-rate metric, not an internally invented number, and it carries no filter at all since the Fiscal Year filter was removed and nothing else applies at that size.

Three fixes made as part of this check: (1) the KPI-size card wasn't implemented — added, using the "overall % Paid" headline the doc specifies. (2) Small was showing one big "Overall received X%" headline plus a single activity's bar, instead of 2-3 bars — one of the two KPI concepts the doc's "What Got Cut" section says was dropped; fixed to show the same progress-bar list every other size uses, and also now forces the default (non-table, non-pie) view at Small the way the doc's "no Switch View" note implies. (3) the Date Range filter's third value didn't match the doc ("Year to Date" vs. the doc's "Custom") and a dead "Year" filter was still present despite the doc saying it was removed — both corrected. Not fixed, flagged instead: Custom Date Range should reveal Beginning/Ending fields per the doc, same open item as W03's Pay Period; and the doc calls this filter "Activity Type" while the render, mock data and citations all consistently call it "Campaign" — worth reconciling one way or the other rather than left mismatched.

Purpose
Shows how much money is currently owed to the organisation in unpaid invoices and how long those invoices have been outstanding, so staff can prioritise which outstanding amounts need attention first.
Customer Research — What We're Working Off
business_center
GlobalData365 — Accounts Receivable Dashboard open_in_new
AR aging dashboards commonly combine a bar or donut chart with a sortable detail table — a pie chart as the sole view is explicitly called out as the wrong choice for aging data, which directly confirms the old design's pie chart was a real defect, already removed here. KPI snapshot tiles above the aging breakdown is a separately recommended combination (Vertaccount) — the basis for the doc's KPI+Bars default, discussed in Logic below.
Logic — What This Shows, and Why We Built It This Way
